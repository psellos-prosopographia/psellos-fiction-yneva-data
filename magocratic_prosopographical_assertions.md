# Magocratic prosopographical assertions (draft)

The following assertions are extracted from chapters 2–5 and phrased for human draft review in the Magocratic narrative layer.

1. Vanadarin is portrayed as the ancestral figure whose line is invoked as the ruling line of Bergthur (the “Vanadarin line”).
2. Marsten ruled Bergthur and its surrounding countryside for seventeen years and died during a harsh winter after contracting a strong fever.
3. Later historians attributed Marsten’s death to a curse by a fleeing Foernumkin chief, though medical reasoning in the narrative suggests meningitis.
4. Marsten’s legitimacy and achievements enabled a stable succession, with only Oundir’s tribe rivaling his family’s prominence.
5. Marsten left a single child, Enka the Younger, as his successor.
6. Enka the Younger is described as a conscientious woman and an able administrator who ruled Bergthur after Marsten.
7. Enka married Borlas the Wide, the only widower chief of a tribe in an active Driyar, to bolster martial legitimacy.
8. Borlas is depicted as congenial and once raucous and lively, later devastated by the deaths of his wife and children in illness or battle.
9. Enka and Borlas’ union was said to be affectionate despite their age gap.
10. Children of Enka and Borlas were to be considered part of the Vanadarin line in Rathic custom.
11. Enka and Borlas had twins: Sylas (male) and Vanyalas (female).
12. Enka expanded aqueducts around Bergthur and oversaw exploration for new water sources during her early reign.
13. Enka extended the Planetarian temple with a hall dedicated to Yneva Thurlaska, an aspect of the star goddess.
14. Enka formalized the priesthood of the Daughters of Yneva, primarily staffed by young aristocratic daughters.
15. Enka promulgated the Enkaian Laws on marriage, dowries, sexual assault, divorce, midwifery, and in-law etiquette, excluding slaves and some servants.
16. Borlas’ battlefield reputation is stated to have reduced pushback against Enka’s reforms.
17. Enka framed her reforms as reinforcing ceremonial duties to the star gods and social duties between genders.
18. Sylas spent his early teens campaigning with his father Borlas and learned horsemanship and weapons.
19. Sylas bonded with soldiers and was scolded by Enka and Vanyalas for his uncouth demeanor at court.
20. As Borlas approached retirement from his last Driyar, Enka ordered Sylas to seek marriage with Himvalda, Oundir’s youngest daughter.
21. Himvalda was betrothed to Sylas at age seven and lived in Vanadarin’s palace as a long-term hostage and nominal fiancée.
22. Oundir accepted the betrothal, portrayed as motivated by trust and respect for Vanadarin’s family.
23. Sylas engaged in extensive carousing and kept mistresses across recently conquered territories while betrothed to Himvalda.
24. Sylas faced Foernumkin overtures of war and led a large army that included over one hundred Armaur in his personal retinue.
25. Hamford the Elder, a Keldarian historian of the 1st century B.R., is cited as a source about Sylas’ conduct and omens.
26. Sylas consulted the high priestess of the Daughters of Yneva for an omen before battle, according to Hamford the Elder.
27. The high priestess read leaves in a ceremonial basin and warned Sylas he would face “the wrath of the deep.”
28. Sylas disregarded the omen and attacked, underestimating Foernumkin forces and Shorkin mercenaries.
29. Sylas led cavalry across the Hvasparsthur river near Feir and was ambushed by slingers and archers.
30. Sylas ordered a retreat after heavy cavalry losses and regrouped with his infantry.
31. Sylas offered silver to Feir and Omagda for reinforcements in the ongoing campaign.
32. Sylas continued campaigning for two years after being hailed Virvelsprins (“Prince of the Vortex”).
33. The title Virvelsprins was bestowed after Sylas’ victory during the First Miracle of Seyet and his return to Bergthur.
34. In 720 B.R., Borlas survived a mild stroke and required daily assistance afterward.
35. Enka announced her intention to abdicate in favor of Sylas and later retired to a villa to care for Borlas.
36. Enka was remembered as a saintly figure and an exemplar of patriotic and familial virtue.
37. Sylas was hailed Rekaz of Bergthur in the spring following Enka’s abdication.
38. Sylas, as Rekaz, sent his advisor Olkin east of the Sylpin River to negotiate with Markinse, Odoanca, and Carpesidhe.
39. Sylas offered his sister Vanyalas’ hand in marriage to Danig, Lord of Markinse, to cement alliance.
40. Sylas styled himself Rekaz of Bergthur to avoid claiming rulership over all Raths, acknowledging the growing power of other city-states.
41. Sylas married Himvalda and she bore him one son before dying shortly after childbirth.
42. Sylas named his son Gronauga, who inherited Himvalda’s emerald green eyes.
43. Sylas summoned Enka and her retinue back to the palace to help raise Gronauga after Himvalda’s death.
44. Borlas died a couple of years before this summons, leaving Enka a widow.
45. Sylas campaigned west of Rathic domains in 712 B.R. and visited Ocazca to requisition Pelkin tribute and pray to Yneva Ereyna.
46. Sylas promised chiefs nearing the end of their Driyars control over conquered southern border lands in exchange for frontier protection.
47. Sylas’ southern campaign resulted in compelling conquered peoples to join Planetarian worship, with their local deities installed in Planetarian temples as Afgudslevin.
48. Sylas’ campaign granted southern border lands to the Ardavir and Welfalir tribes.
49. Princess Enka continued to influence government after abdication, provoking a coalition of chiefs.
50. A Daughter of Yneva attempted to assassinate Enka during a ritual but only wounded her forearm.
51. Enka ordered her maidens to keep the assassination attempt secret and later made concessions to chiefs over plunder.
52. Sylas led a six‑month campaign across Rathic settlements, rewarding voluntary submission and imposing harsh tribute on resistance.
53. Sylas is depicted as exercising tight control of his army, preventing revolts or mass destruction.
54. Sylas met an independent chieftain descended from one of Vanadarin’s original companions and forced him to kneel, per a notable anecdote.
55. Sylas’ reputation after the Miracle of Seyet aided his diplomacy and contributed to towns surrendering without fight.
56. Sylas sent envoys to northern Rathic tribes and signed a letter as “Sylas Virvelsprins, Rekaz of Bergthur.”
57. The Rekaz of Omagda sent his son Noerlinga to lead an attack on Thuride.
58. Noerlinga died in battle, prompting the Rekaz of Omagda to surrender and commit suicide.
59. Sylas granted the former Rekaz of Omagda a Planetarian funeral and installed a pro‑Bergthurian noble as successor.
60. Sylas offered silver to chiefs in the last year of their Driyar to remain as a soft garrison in Omagda.
61. Sylas allowed Markinse to take control of Umba after its surrender.
62. Sylas returned toward Bergthur and negotiated with chiefs of inactive Driyars who sought recognition and reward.
63. Sylas announced three new prestige positions for inactive Driyar chiefs: Master of Strategy, Master of Banners, and Master of Supply.
64. Masters of Strategy were to serve as general commanders answering only to the prince and eligible only after active Driyar service.
65. The Master of Banners was tasked with enforcing standardization and discipline while incorporating Planetarian banners into regiments.
66. The Master of Supply was to coordinate logistics between active and passive armies and be elected by active Driyar chiefs.
67. The Feast of the Boundary is associated with rituals honoring Tyvas Ynavjan, an aspect of the Rathic god of the sky.
68. King Uncalyn is named as the founder, in Kelpic tradition, of the Uskelpes city‑state on the Pyg.
69. Prince Marsten contacted Cappu to acquire armaments for his campaign against the Foernumkin, causing controversy in the Uskelpes league.
70. Gronauga, son of Sylas, grew up in the marble palace while his father campaigned.
71. Gronauga preferred scholarly study of nature and astronomy and was regarded as modest and serious.
72. Gronauga often accompanied Princess Enka to the Planetarian temple and was nicknamed Litenstjarn (“Little Star”) for his green eyes.
73. Gronauga befriended a slave boy named Caradunir at the Planetarian temple and the friendship was lifelong.
74. Caradunir was a slave boy assigned by an influential clan to work with the Daughters of Yneva.
75. Sylas disapproved of Gronauga’s bookish tendencies but allowed them to continue so long as martial training progressed.
76. Olkinadir, son of Olkin, served as Gronauga’s personal trainer during Sylas’ campaigns.
77. The Dagnafir chieftain Ludergyos and his son Borindas died after the Battle of Shorokyatar.
78. Sylas supported Borifson, a cousin of Borindas, as the new chieftain of the diminished Dagnafir tribe.
79. Sylas established a new border east of Omagda after defeating the Foernumkin, but his meddling in Dagnafir leadership caused resentment.
80. Silpir, chief of the Sanarkin tribe, confronted Sylas and accused him of recklessness during the prolonged winter campaign.
